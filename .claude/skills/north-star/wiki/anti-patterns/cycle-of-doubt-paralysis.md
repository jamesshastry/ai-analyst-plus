# Cycle of doubt paralysis

## TL;DR
Teams converging on an NSM bounce through a three-step cycle of doubt: "yes, that idea makes sense" → "but how would we measure?" → "wait, we want something else entirely." Without a 'good enough for now' stop rule, they spin indefinitely and never ship an NSM.

## Spot signals
- The team is in workshop #3+ and still has no candidate.
- Every convergence is followed by "wait, what about…" that resets the discussion.
- The bar for an acceptable NSM keeps rising as the team iterates.
- The team doesn't realize their first pass IS a hypothesis, not a final answer.

## Fix recipe
1. Reframe the first pass as a hypothesis about levers of growth — uncertainty is the point, not a bug (Cutler, p. 23).
2. Aim for "directionally accurate," then adapt as you learn — the NSM will change.
3. Set a stop rule: pick the best candidate after N iterations and commit to running with it for a quarter.
4. Borrow John's PRO TIP (p. 33): "Be willing to just try on a North Star for size. When you get one that's halfway decent, throw it in there. Pick one, work on inputs, and then you can go back and iterate."

## Examples
- Opposite trap: [Rushing the statement exercise](rushing-statement-exercise.md) — under-investing in iteration vs. over-investing.
- Related: [Attachment to a stale NSM](attachment-to-nsm.md) — once teams ship an NSM, they often swing the other way and refuse to change it.
