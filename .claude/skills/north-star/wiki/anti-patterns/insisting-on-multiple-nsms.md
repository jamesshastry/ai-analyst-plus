# Insisting on multiple North Star Metrics

## TL;DR
Adopting two or more NSMs because internal product lines feel different defeats the framework — it reproduces the cross-team in-fighting and resource battles the single-NSM model exists to dissolve. The boundaries usually come from the org chart, not from how customers experience the offering.

## Spot signals
- Teams argue that their product lines are too different to share an NSM.
- Each product team proposes its own NSM in the workshop.
- The boundaries between "products" are drawn from the org chart or P&L, not from how customers perceive the offering.
- Cross-team prioritization disputes persist after the framework is adopted.
- Workshop refrain: "We can't possibly have one North Star because we're delivering apples and they're delivering pants."

## Fix recipe
1. Re-anchor on Ted Clark's diagnostic: "Do customers view these as distinct products? Or do they view [the company] as a single product?" (p. 41 bank example).
2. Look for real boundaries in users, needs, and strategy — not in org structure.
3. Note where supposed "products" are actually packages or add-ons within one larger product.
4. Reserve multiple NSMs for the genuine exception: distinct lines of business with different customer bases and P&Ls (p. 11).
5. Re-walk the fundamentals: the NSM is the proof test for cross-team investment decisions — that only works if there's one.

## Examples
- **Bank case (p. 41):** Different product lines argued for their own NSMs. Ted's question — do customers see the bank as one product? — collapsed the multi-NSM argument.
- **Contested:** Some practitioners argue multi-product platforms (e.g., Google) genuinely need multiple NSMs. See debate page.
