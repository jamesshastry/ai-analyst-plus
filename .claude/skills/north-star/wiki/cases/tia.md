# Tia — Three Languages and the NSF Workshop

## TL;DR
Tia is a narrative case (anonymized B2B SaaS product designer/PM) used in the playbook's introduction to demonstrate why teams need the North Star Framework. After a workshop, she realized her team had been speaking three disconnected languages — customer, product, business — that the NSF tied together. She continued using the framework, advanced her career, and adopted NSF at a new company.

## Story
Tia is the playbook's opening narrative case — anonymized, but presented as a real workshop attendee. When the authors met her at a North Star workshop in New York City, she had been a product designer and PM at an enterprise B2B SaaS company for four years and "was feeling burned out." She had done everything a product person should do — research, advocacy, outcomes over outputs — but her company kept cycling through shiny objects, success theater, and false starts [case-study-fragment-p004-l0067-tia-burnout-context].

Through the workshop, Tia surfaced the diagnosis the playbook uses to motivate the whole framework. She realized her team had been speaking three languages with nothing to connect them: (1) the language of the customer — needs, goals, experiences, delight; (2) the language of the product — features, workflows, releases; and (3) the language of the business — vision, differentiation, revenue, growth. The NSF, in her telling, was the artifact that finally connected the three perspectives [case-study-fragment-p005-l0083-tia-three-languages-insight].

Tia's outcome closes the introduction. She continued using the skills and tools she learned at the workshop; her career progressed; and she introduced the framework at a new company, where she successfully adapted it to a new product, team, and development process. She reported better conversations, more alignment, and more impact [case-study-fragment-p005-l0104-tia-outcome-progression].

The case is structurally different from the other named-company cases in the wiki: it does not pin a specific NSM or input architecture; instead, it demonstrates what the framework does for the person inside the org when adoption goes well.

## Why it works
The Tia case anchors the playbook's framing that the NSF is fundamentally an alignment artifact, not a metric. The "three languages" diagnosis is the case's payload: the most common product-org dysfunction is not lack of effort or lack of taste, it's the absence of a shared object that lets customer, product, and business decisions resolve to the same thing. Tia's outcome (re-adoption at a second company) makes the case for the framework's portability across orgs.

## What broke
The case is single-source by design — Tia is anonymized, and her company is not named. There is no path to external verification, which is appropriate for an introductory narrative case but caps confidence at medium. The playbook does not document the specific NSM Tia's teams adopted or whether the second-company adoption produced a measurable business outcome.
