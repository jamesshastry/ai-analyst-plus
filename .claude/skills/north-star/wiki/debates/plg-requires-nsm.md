# Does PLG actually require an NSM?

## TL;DR
The playbook says PLG companies *especially* need an NSM; without one they decay into feature factories. The founder-taste counter-stance: several poster-child PLG companies (Linear, Vercel, Notion early days, Figma pre-IPO) operated for years without a publicly-articulated NSM — their organizing primitive was sharp founder product taste plus a fast-shipping team. Both sides agree that at scale (>100 PMs, multi-product) some shared metric is required. They disagree on whether the framework adds value or ceremony at <50 PMs.

## Where they agree
- At scale, a shared metric becomes necessary to coordinate multiple teams.
- Feature-factory dysfunction is real and deadly [definition-p013-l0306-feature-factory].
- A poorly-chosen NSM is worse than no NSM (the wall-proclamation anti-pattern [anti-pattern-p044-l1028-nsm-as-wall-proclamation]).

## Arguments for the playbook stance
- The PLG-needs-NSM atom argues a product-led company without a shared definition of customer value ships features whose business case is "this is what we built" and decays into a feature factory [concept-p013-l0300-plg-needs-nsm].
- PLG by definition pushes growth into the product itself, which means every product decision is a growth decision — and growth decisions without a shared metric are governance theater [definition-p013-l0300-product-led-growth].
- The framework's adoption recipe is harder to retrofit at Series C than to install at Series A; founders who delay NSM articulation pay a culture-transformation tax later.
- The act of arguing about which metric represents value is itself the strategic conversation the framework exists to provoke — even if the answer is wrong, the conversation is valuable.

## Arguments against (founder-taste lineage)
- Linear, Vercel, Notion (early), and arguably Figma (pre-IPO) operated for years without a publicly-articulated NSM. Their organizing primitive was sharp founder product taste plus a small fast-shipping team that did not need a framework to coordinate.
- In PLG companies with strong founder-product fit and a small team, an NSM is *post-hoc rationalization* of decisions the founders are already making well. Imposing the framework adds ceremony without adding judgment.
- A premature NSM in a founder-led company can produce false convergence: the team commits to a number the founders would have overridden when their taste said otherwise, and the override now feels like a process violation.
- The "feature factory" failure mode is a function of *team size and founder-distance*, not an absence of an NSM — small teams close to the founder are not feature factories even without a framework.
- **No Tier-A/B essay defending "founder taste over NSM framework" was located within counter-research's budget.** The position is dominant in seed-stage operator and VC circles but not crystallized in a citable piece.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook above ~50 PMs / Series B, leans founder-taste below. The two positions are not flatly opposed: every founder-taste shop eventually scales past the point where taste cannot coordinate the org, at which point the framework becomes load-bearing. The substantive question is *when* to install the NSM. The playbook's default ("as soon as you have a product") is too early for many founder-led PLG companies; the founder-taste default ("never, the founder decides") breaks at scale. A wiki-level improvement: name a population threshold (≥50 PMs, ≥2 product surfaces, ≥1 layer between founder and IC) as the trigger for NSM articulation, rather than "as soon as you have a product."

## Why this matters
Determines whether founders invest workshop time at Series A or wait until Series C. Affects whether early PLG teams view the framework as helpful structure or as ceremony imported from larger companies that doesn't fit their stage.
