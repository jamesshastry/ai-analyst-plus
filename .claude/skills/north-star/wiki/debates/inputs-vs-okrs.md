# Inputs vs. OKRs — separate frameworks or the same thing?

## TL;DR
The playbook says the NSF is explicitly *not* OKRs and *not* MBOs. Inputs are descriptive leverage points; OKRs sit alongside as a separate goal-setting practice. Christina Wodtke argues Key Results must BE metric outcomes — which positions input metrics as the right KRs and creates redundancy if you maintain both. Both sides want measurable, value-aligned metrics. They disagree on whether the tree and the OKR doc should be one artifact or two.

## Where they agree
- A single guiding metric should anchor the strategy.
- The operational layer (KRs / inputs) must be measurable, not project lists.
- Teams checking to-do lists instead of watching metrics have institutionalized self-delusion (Wodtke's phrase) — both sides reject this.

## Arguments for the playbook stance (separate artifacts)
- The NSF is explicitly not OKRs and not MBOs [concept-p019-l0436-what-nsf-is-not] — different audiences, different cadences, different review meetings.
- Inputs are descriptive (independent variables that move the dependent NSM) [concept-p009-l0190-inputs-independent-nsm-dependent], not contractual targets — calling them KRs would convert them into performance-management artifacts and change the conversation.
- Ethan Garr writes in *How to Win with OKRs and a North Star Metric* (Breakout Growth, Tier B): *"OKRs are a tactical process that helps teams align, while a North Star Metric reflects important principles of growth."* ([Breakout Growth](https://breakoutgrowth.net/2021/01/26/how-to-win-with-okrs-and-a-north-star-metric/))
- Separating description (the tree) from commitment (the OKR doc) preserves the right to revise either without political fallout — the tree can update when new diagnostic data arrives, the OKRs can renegotiate at quarterly cadence.
- The inputs-are-leverage-points framing [concept-p027-l0603-inputs-are-leverage-points] is descriptive; KR-collapsing converts leverage into commitment, which over-constrains experimentation.

## Arguments against (KR-collapsing)
- Christina Wodtke, *Why Key Results Need to Be Results*: *"If you make a Key Result a project, you are locked into it even if it doesn't work. … Once your team is checking to-do lists instead of watching metrics, you've institutionalized self-delusion. … If a result is a result, you can change your tactics until the numbers move. If a result is a project, you have locked yourself into achieving it, even if you find out it's the wrong approach."* ([cwodtke.medium.com](https://cwodtke.medium.com/why-key-results-need-to-be-results-7a223e6752b6) — Tier A)
- In Wodtke's frame, the input metrics of the NSF are exactly the right candidates to BE the KRs — they are measurable customer-value-aligned numbers the team is investing to move.
- Maintaining two parallel metric stacks creates ceremony tax and confusion about which document is authoritative when they disagree.
- Large-co OKR practice routinely collapses inputs into KRs because the alternative — running both — produces duplicated work and contradictory targets.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook on intent, leans Wodtke on operational reality. Both authors agree on substance: a measurable, customer-value-aligned metric should drive team behavior, and that metric should be the moveable target. The disagreement is artifact-level. The playbook's insistence on separateness is defensible for organizations where the metric tree serves a longer time horizon than quarterly OKRs (the tree is strategic; the KRs are tactical), but in practice many shops collapse them and lose nothing. A wiki-level improvement: acknowledge the collapse pattern is legitimate and provide a decision rule for when to keep them separate (multi-year strategy + quarterly OKRs) vs. when to collapse (quarterly strategy refresh + KRs = inputs).

## Why this matters
Affects whether the NSM tree is a strategy artifact (lived in by leadership) or a performance-management artifact (lived in by every team) — different organizational politics, different review cadences, different document maintenance burdens.
