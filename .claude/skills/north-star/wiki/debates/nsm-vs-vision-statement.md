# NSM vs. North Star vision-statement

## TL;DR
The playbook is metric-centric: a number, its inputs, and the bets that ladder to them. A vision can *inform* the NSM, but the NSM is the organizing artifact. Geoffrey Moore and Marty Cagan treat the *narrative* (a 3-5 year story of the future product) as the unifying artifact. The metric is a thermometer; the story is the compass. The playbook partly concedes this via the "North Star of the North Star" line about conversation quality.

## Where they agree
- A vision statement is valuable — the playbook explicitly says it informs the NSM [concept-p037-l0840-vision-statement-informs-nsm].
- Three languages of teams (customer, product, business) need a shared anchor [concept-p005-l0083-three-languages-of-teams].
- Conversation quality matters more than any single artifact — the playbook's "North Star of the North Star" atom concedes that the metric is downstream of the language and beliefs teams share [concept-p060-l0396-north-star-of-the-north-star].
- A bad metric and a bad vision both produce coordination failure.

## Arguments for the playbook stance (metric-centric)
- A metric is testable; a narrative is not. The NSM has a value each week that can be reviewed, debated, and acted on.
- The metric tree gives a shared language across customer/product/business that a narrative cannot — narratives translate differently for each audience.
- The tree is a scaffold for conversation, not a prison [concept-p007-l0153-nsf-tree-is-scaffold]; it accommodates narrative work above it and discovery work below it.
- Operating reviews need numbers; a pure narrative gets re-interpreted every quarter and never produces accountability.
- The framework's worked examples (Spotify, Burger King, Netflix) all anchor on a behavioral metric that integrated their narrative into something measurable.

## Arguments against (narrative-centric, Cagan / Moore lineage)
- Marty Cagan's product-vision work (Inspired, Empowered) treats a 3-5 year narrative as the unifying artifact: vivid, specific, opinionated about the future the team is trying to build.
- Geoffrey Moore's strategic-discipline lineage (Crossing the Chasm, Zone to Win) argues that the *story* is what aligns talent and capital; numbers are abstract.
- The strong version: numbers are easy to game, hard to recruit talent against, and decay in meaning over time (see Goodhart debate). A vivid product vision moves humans in a way a metric cannot.
- A metric-centric NSF wins the operating review but loses the recruiting meeting — the candidate who joins because of the narrative is different from the one who joins because of the metric.
- **No Tier-A/B essay directly critiquing the NSF as "too metric-centric" was located within counter-research's budget.** Cagan's and Moore's work are canonical in product/strategy but do not specifically target the NSF framing.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook for operating reviews, leans narrative for recruiting and 5-year strategy. The two positions are complementary more than opposed: most mature product orgs maintain both a 3-5 year vision narrative (for recruiting, strategy reviews, board decks) and an NSM tree (for quarterly operating reviews). The playbook's "vision informs NSM" atom is exactly this synthesis. The substantive question is which artifact a founder defends to a new exec hire on day one — and which artifact gets re-read when the team is lost. A wiki-level improvement: explicitly position the product vision as a *peer* artifact to the NSM tree, not a subordinate one, and provide guidance on when each gets used.

## Why this matters
Affects what the founder shows the next exec hire on day one. Affects which artifact gets defended in a strategic offsite. Affects whether the team responds to a flat-NSM quarter with a roadmap revision (metric-centric) or a vision revisit (narrative-centric).
