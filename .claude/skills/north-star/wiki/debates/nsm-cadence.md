# How long should an NSM last?

## TL;DR
The playbook says revisit the NSM when the market changes, the business reaches a new stage, or the metric proves uncontrollable — the directional-revenue test is the trigger. The implicit cadence is multi-year. OKR-fluent shops argue NSMs should evolve with strategy (annually or even quarterly) because strategy shifts change what "value" means. Both sides agree NSMs do change. They disagree on default cadence: rarely-with-reluctance (playbook) vs. routinely-with-strategy-refresh (OKR view).

## Where they agree
- NSMs do change — the playbook explicitly allows it [concept-p045-l0054-when-to-change-the-nsm].
- A frequently-changing NSM erodes its function as a coordinating artifact.
- An attachment-to-the-NSM anti-pattern exists: refusing to change when the business has materially shifted is its own failure mode [anti-pattern-p045-l1058-attachment-to-nsm].

## Arguments for the playbook stance (multi-year stability)
- The framework's adoption recipe (sponsor, leadership buy-in, change management, onboarding, approvals) assumes the NSM is stable enough to teach new hires [concept-p044-l0032-make-the-nsm-stick].
- An NSM that changes every year cannot anchor a culture; the "signals NSF is working" atoms describe years-long behavior shifts that require persistence [concept-p043-l0011-signals-nsf-is-working].
- The directional-revenue test (NSM flat but revenue moving, or vice versa) is a principled trigger that fires only when the metric has lost its leading-indicator power — not when strategy nuances shift.
- Frequent NSM changes signal the team has not done the upfront work to find a durable metric; the answer is better discovery, not more change.

## Arguments against (strategy-cadence)
- OKR-fluent organizations refresh their North Star artifacts annually or quarterly because strategy shifts (new segment, new business model, new platform) change what value means.
- A static multi-year NSM becomes a legacy artifact that everyone respects but nobody believes, especially after a pivot or a new exec arrives.
- The "stick" recipe creates change-management resistance: by the time the directional-revenue test fires, the team has spent years optimizing the wrong thing.
- Lenny's community on strategy refreshes argues that strategy is a quarterly conversation, and metrics should be aligned with it.
- **No Tier-A/B essay arguing for explicit annual/quarterly NSM refresh was located within counter-research's budget.** The view is dominant in OKR-shop operator circles but not crystallized in a single canonical piece.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook for products with stable value propositions, leans strategy-cadence for products in volatile markets or post-pivot. Both positions have defensible failure modes: too-stable NSMs become faith documents (the playbook's own attachment-to-NSM anti-pattern); too-fluid NSMs cannot anchor cross-team coordination (the playbook's "make it stick" prescription). A wiki-level improvement: name the trigger conditions for change more crisply than "the market changed," and pair the directional-revenue test with an explicit annual NSM-coupling audit (see related Goodhart debate).

## Why this matters
Affects whether changing the NSM feels like a betrayal of culture (playbook view) or a routine strategic refresh (OKR view). Different organizational politics; different leadership-meeting energy when the change is proposed.
