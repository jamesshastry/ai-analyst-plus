# Gap thinking vs. present thinking — false binary?

## TL;DR
The playbook says gap thinking (fixate on a future endpoint, plan the path) fails because the endpoint never materializes and devalues the present. Present thinking (work the inputs now, learn forward) wins. The scenario-planning and strategy tradition (Roger Martin's *Playing to Win*, Geoffrey Moore) argues that abandoning the future endpoint produces tactically-busy organizations with no destination — mature strategy requires a far-future vision (5-10 years) so present-day input choices have a frame. Both reject naive 5-year roadmaps with milestone precision.

## Where they agree
- Naive 5-year roadmaps with month-precision milestones are fantasy [concept-p046-l0084-gap-thinking-fails-because-future-never-arrives].
- Working the inputs is real product work, not pre-work [definition-p046-l0088-present-thinking].
- Vision statements have a role and inform the NSM [concept-p037-l0840-vision-statement-informs-nsm].
- Both reject the failure mode where teams plan in detail for a future that is too uncertain to plan for.

## Arguments for the playbook stance (present thinking)
- Gap thinking fails because the future endpoint never arrives [concept-p046-l0084-gap-thinking-fails-because-future-never-arrives] — the team builds toward a fantasy and devalues real signal from the present.
- Present thinking treats the inputs as the unit of attention: move them, learn from the response, update the tree [definition-p046-l0088-present-thinking].
- The playbook does not abandon vision — it explicitly says vision informs the NSM [concept-p037-l0840-vision-statement-informs-nsm]. The dichotomy is about which artifact organizes weekly work, not whether vision exists.
- In practice, gap-thinking organizations spend disproportionate energy on the gap document (5-year strategic plan, 3-year roadmap) and underinvest in the present-input feedback loop where actual learning happens.
- The framework's "two categories of trouble" and "cycle of doubt" atoms describe the dysfunctions of teams that cannot focus on the present.

## Arguments against (mature strategy needs both)
- Roger Martin's *Playing to Win* argues that strategy is fundamentally about *choices* — and choices require a destination. Without a far-future vision, present-day input choices have no frame.
- Geoffrey Moore's strategic-discipline lineage similarly treats the future-state narrative as load-bearing for direction.
- The strong version: "present thinking" can degenerate into local optimization, where every quarter looks good on its inputs but the company has no theory of where it is going.
- Some gap is what makes a strategy a strategy. Closing the gap is the work; eliminating the gap eliminates the strategy.
- The playbook's "present thinking wins" framing risks being read as license to abandon long-horizon planning entirely, which is not what mature strategy practice does.
- **No Tier-A/B essay directly critiquing the playbook's gap/present framing was located within counter-research's budget.** Martin and Moore are canonical but their critiques are general strategy literature, not NSF-specific.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook on operational discipline, leans Martin/Moore on strategic framing. The dichotomy is partly a false binary: mature product orgs run both a long-horizon vision (gap, in Martin's vocabulary) and a present-input feedback loop, and the two artifacts serve different purposes. The playbook's framing is best read as a corrective to organizations over-invested in gap documents and under-invested in input movement — not as a prohibition on long-horizon vision. A wiki-level improvement: explicitly position present thinking as the *weekly* operating discipline and gap thinking (or vision-as-frame) as the *annual* strategic discipline, with the NSM tree as the bridge.

## Why this matters
Affects whether the NSF feels strategically vacant to a CEO who wants a 5-year picture. Affects whether the team's weekly cadence is dominated by long-horizon planning (gap default) or present-input experimentation (playbook default).
