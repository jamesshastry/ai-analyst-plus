# Should the NSM be public to customers?

## TL;DR
The playbook treats socialization as internal — the adoption recipe (sponsor, leadership buy-in, change management, onboarding, approvals) is all org-facing. Publishing the NSM to customers is not discussed. Some companies publish their NSM or close analogues (Buffer's open metrics, Spotify's public talks about "time spent listening," Stripe's transparent goals). The argument: external publication forces internal honesty and creates accountability. The counter: it leaks strategy, invites criticism on dips, and converts an internal organizing tool into a marketing asset.

## Where they agree
- The NSM should not be a marketing slogan even when shared externally [anti-pattern-p044-l1028-nsm-as-wall-proclamation].
- Socialization inside the org is necessary [concept-p033-l0740-socialize-your-nsm].
- The NSM needs to stick — be remembered, used, defended internally [concept-p044-l0032-make-the-nsm-stick].

## Arguments for the playbook stance (internal-only)
- The NSM is an organizing artifact, not a marketing asset. Publishing it externally changes its function in a way that distorts its design.
- External publication invites criticism every time the number dips, which produces pressure to redefine the metric to look good — a Goodhart-adjacent failure mode.
- It telegraphs strategy to competitors. A competitor who knows your NSM knows what you are optimizing and can position against it.
- The adoption recipe (sponsor, change management, onboarding) is built for internal coordination, not for managing an external audience.
- Customer-facing communication should be about customer outcomes, not internal coordination metrics. The translation from NSM to "what we promise users" is a separate exercise.

## Arguments against (external transparency)
- Publishing the NSM forces internal honesty — the team cannot quietly redefine the metric without external embarrassment.
- It recruits aligned customers who choose the product because they share its value definition (Buffer's transparency dashboard is a recruiting tool for users who value openness).
- It creates external accountability that internal-only socialization lacks — a public number cannot be quietly retired when it becomes inconvenient.
- Spotify's open discussion of "time spent listening" did not visibly hurt them strategically, despite the obvious risk; the transparency reinforced their narrative.
- Stripe and Patagonia's transparent goal statements are recruiting and brand assets, not strategic leaks.
- **No Tier-A/B essay advocating "publish your NSM" was located within counter-research's budget.** The position is well-attested via example companies but not crystallized in a single load-bearing piece.

## Current consensus
Weak — leans playbook for most companies, leans transparency for companies whose brand depends on openness (Buffer, GitLab, some open-source businesses). The two positions are not flatly opposed: external transparency is a strategic choice with real tradeoffs, and the playbook does not prohibit it — it just does not address it. The substantive question is whether the company's brand and competitive position make external NSM publication a net force-for-good. For most enterprise and B2B companies, the strategic-leak risk dominates; for transparency-branded consumer companies, the accountability benefit dominates. A wiki-level improvement: name the conditions under which external NSM publication is defensible (brand built on transparency; metric is not competitively sensitive; org has the maturity to handle public scrutiny of dips).

## Why this matters
Different organizations; different talent and customer dynamics. Affects whether the NSM is treated as confidential strategy material or as a public commitment device.
